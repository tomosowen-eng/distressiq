The financial ratios

Each company's most recent quarterly (or half-yearly) balance sheet and income statement is used to compute four standard credit/liquidity ratios:

Current Ratio

Current assets divided by current liabilities. Answers: can this company cover what it owes in the next 12 months with what it can turn into cash in the next 12 months? Below 1.0x means current liabilities exceed current assets — a potential short-term liquidity squeeze, though common and often unremarkable for retailers and other businesses with fast inventory turnover.

Debt-to-Equity

Total debt divided by shareholder equity. A measure of leverage — how much of the company is financed by borrowing versus its own capital. Higher values mean more financial risk if earnings decline, since debt obligations don't shrink when revenue does.

Net Debt / EBITDA

Total debt minus cash, divided by EBITDA (earnings before interest, tax, depreciation and amortisation). Roughly: how many years of current earnings would it take to pay off all debt, net of cash on hand. Above 3–4x is generally considered elevated leverage; above 6x is a level that would concern most credit analysts.

Interest Coverage

EBIT (earnings before interest and tax) divided by interest expense. Answers: how comfortably can current earnings cover the interest bill alone (before even touching principal)? Below 2x is thin — a meaningful earnings decline could leave the company unable to service its debt from operating earnings.

The Altman Z-Score

The Altman Z-Score is a composite distress score first published in 1968, combining five weighted balance-sheet and income-statement ratios (working capital, retained earnings, EBIT, market value of equity, and revenue, each scaled by total assets or liabilities) into a single number:

  • Safe — Z-Score above 2.99
  • Grey — Z-Score between 1.81 and 2.99, an ambiguous zone
  • Distress — Z-Score below 1.81

Why it's marked N/A for Financials and Real Estate. The original formula was built and calibrated on public industrial and manufacturing companies. Banks, insurers, exchanges, and REITs have fundamentally different balance sheet structures — for example, a bank's "debt" is largely customer deposits, not leverage in the distress sense the formula assumes. Applying the formula to these sectors routinely produces scores that look alarming but reflect the business model, not financial distress. DistressIQ suppresses the zone label for Financials and Real Estate constituents rather than show a misleading reading — the underlying raw score is still available in each company's detail panel, and the AI risk rating (not the Z-Score) is the headline signal for those companies.

How the AI risk assessment works

For each company, an AI model (Claude) is given the current ratios above plus up to three years of quarterly history — revenue, EBIT, total debt, cash, and net debt — and asked to write a structured assessment rather than just restate the numbers. The model returns five fields:

  • Risk Rating — Low, Moderate, Elevated, or High
  • Confidence — Low, Medium, or High, with a stated reason
  • Primary Driver — the specific mechanism behind the rating (a solvency problem, an earnings shock, a liquidity timing issue, a governance flag, or a cost blowout), not a generic restatement of the ratios
  • Trend — the direction of travel across the historical periods shown, and whether deterioration (if any) looks acute or gradual
  • Watch Trigger — a specific, concrete event or threshold that would change the assessment

The model is explicitly instructed to be decisive and specific rather than hedge — but see the data-quality safeguard below for the one case where it's instructed to do the opposite.

Data-quality safeguards

Fundamentals data aggregated from public filings is not always clean. DistressIQ found a real example: one company's reported total debt appeared to jump by roughly 55% in a single period with no corresponding event, which turned out to be a lease-liability figure counted twice in the underlying data provider's field for that one period — not an actual financial event.

The AI prompt now includes an explicit check: before writing its assessment, the model scans the historical data for any single-period move greater than 50% in a major line item that isn't explained elsewhere in the data. When it finds one, it is instructed to name the possibility of a data-quality or reporting artifact directly, cap its Confidence at Medium, and point its Watch Trigger at confirming the figure — rather than inventing a specific cause like "a major acquisition" to explain a number it can't actually verify. Where this applies, it's visible directly in that company's Confidence reasoning and flagged in the results table.

Data source and update cadence

Fundamentals (balance sheet, income statement, and company reference data) are sourced from EODHD. The full pipeline — fetching fundamentals, computing ratios, and generating each AI write-up — runs automatically once a week and republishes this site with the latest results. Individual companies may report quarterly or half-yearly depending on their own disclosure schedule, so the "as of" statement date shown in each detail panel can differ company to company even within the same weekly run.

DistressIQ is an educational and portfolio project. All risk ratings, confidence levels, and written analysis are generated by an AI model from public financial data and are not reviewed by a human analyst. Nothing on this page is financial advice, a research report, or a recommendation to buy, sell, or hold any security. Ratios and scores (including the Altman Z-Score) may be unreliable for certain sectors — see each company's detail panel for context.
